\section{Introduction}

Computer chess has a very long history, dating back to the earliest days of computer science \citep{silver-2017}. It is also ideal for developing artificial intelligence \citep{tang-2024, toshniwal-2022}. Programs, called \textit{chess engines} such as Stockfish have achieved superhuman performance, utilizing various techniques such as Monte-Carlo Tree Search, and customized neural networks \citep{thestockfishdevelopers-2026}.

The Transformer architecture \citep{vaswani-2017} is a very influential architecture, used for the majority of language models. It has seen use in natural language processing, vision, and even analysis of chess itself \citep{ruoss-2024,monroe-2024}. \citet{radford-2019} showed that language models, if trained on a "sufficiently large and diverse dataset", can perform well across many domains and natural-language tasks (such as translation, text ).

In this paper, we build and analyze Transformer-based generative (decoder-only) models at the ability to synthetically generate chess games.

The source code and \LaTeX{} source of this paper are open-source at \url{https://github.com/duc25519/chess-transformer-project/}. The dataset used in training and testing is the UCI-processed version of the Lichess open databases by \citet{davis-2024}.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Definitions} \label{sec:definitions}

\subsubsection{Chess Game and Categories}

A \textit{chess game} is a finite sequence of \textit{half-moves} \(h_1, h_2, \dots, h_n\) (also called \textit{plies}) from the standard initial position of chess. Each half-move is represented using Universal Chess Interface (\textit{UCI}) notation, in which each move is defined by picking up a piece in a \textit{starting square}, and placing it in the \textit{destination square}. 

In this paper, we define a \textit{valid} chess game to be a chess game with no invalid moves, and equivalently, an \textit{invalid} chess game to be a chess game with at least one invalid move.

For valid chess games, we also define the two following smaller categories:

\begin{itemize}
    \item A \textit{complete} chess game is a chess game that terminates in a decisive condition under the FIDE Laws of Chess \citep{fide-2023}: checkmate, stalemate, insufficient material, the fifty-move rule, or threefold repetition.
    \item An \textit{incomplete} chess game is a chess game that does not meet the above conditions. This includes games ended by resignation or mutual agreement, whose terminal conditions cannot be inferred from the move sequence alone.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Legal Mask} \label{sec:legal-mask}

Language models are \textit{stochastic}: they sample each token from a learned probability distribution, and may produce tokens that correspond to illegal moves. Furthermore, despite the large amount of moves in the vocabulary, the amount of legal moves in a position on average is only approximately \(30\) \citep{shannon-1950}. To address this, we introduce a \textit{legal mask}, which restricts sampling at each step to the set of moves that are legal given the current board state.

% fuck it

Formally, let \(\mathcal{V}\) denote the move vocabulary and let \(\mathcal{L}(h_1, \dots, h_n) \subseteq \mathcal{V}\) denote the set of  legal moves given the board state induced by the sequence \(h_1, \dots, h_n\). The model produces logits  \(\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{V}|}\); the legal mask sets the logits of illegal moves to \(-\infty\) before sampling:

\begin{equation}
    \tilde{z}_v =
    \begin{cases}
        z_v & \text{if } v \in \mathcal{L}(h_1, \dots, h_n) \\
        -\infty & \text{otherwise}
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

The next move is then sampled from the renormalized distribution:

\begin{equation}
    P(h_{n+1} = v \mid h_1, \dots, h_n)
    = \text{softmax}(\tilde{\mathbf{z}})_v
    = \frac{\exp(z_v)}{\displaystyle\sum_{u \,\in\,
    \mathcal{L}(h_1, \dots, h_n)} \exp(z_u)}
\end{equation}

Since \(\exp(-\infty) = 0\), illegal moves contribute nothing to the denominator, and the distribution renormalizes automatically over legal moves only.

The legal mask will only guarantee generation of a next valid half-move \(h_{n + 1}\) given existing half-moves \(h_1, h_2, \dots, h_n\). It does not guarantee game completion, lower centipawn-loss, etc.

We evaluate the model on the task of \textit{synthetic game generation}. More specifically, we perform \textit{causal language modeling}, by attempting to generate a full game starting from a sequence of (potentially empty) opening half-moves.

The games will be generated with and without the use of the legal mask. For each case, We generate \(1000\) games using models with and without the legal mask, and in both cases, we evaluate the game length (in number of moves), completeness (as defined above). For the case of generation without a legal mask, game validity is also evaluated.

\subsubsection{Average Centipawn Loss}

Let \(P\) be a position, and \(f: P \mapsto \mathbb{R}\) to be an \textit{evaluation function}. The higher \(f(P)\) is, the more likely White is to win, and vice versa. Let \(H_P\) be the set of legal half-moves in \(P\). For any legal half-move \(h \in H_P\), let \(P \mid h\) be the position achieved by performing the move \(h\) in \(P\).

Let \(h^* \in H_P\) to be the best possible half-move. Then the \textit{centipawn loss} is defined to be the following:

\[
\text{CPL}(P, h) = 
\begin{cases}
100 \cdot \max(0, f(P \mid h^*) - f(P \mid h)) &\text{if the half-move is done by White} \\
100 \cdot \max(0, f(P \mid h) - f(P \mid h^*)) &\text{if the half-move is done by Black}
\end{cases}
\]

The Average Centipawn Loss (\textit{ACPL}) is defined as the average CPL of all half-moves a player made throughout the span of one game.

One central limitation of this work lies in the dataset.
